Anxiety in Adolescents

DSM‑5 Types · Screening · CBT · Nursing Care | Adele Pillitteri, 7e

Overview & Epidemiology

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions in adolescents, characterized by excessive fear or worry that interferes with daily functioning. Globally, ~1 in 7 adolescents (10‑19 years) experience a mental health condition, with anxiety disorders accounting for a large proportion. In the Philippines, prevalence of anxiety symptoms among adolescents is estimated at 5‑10%, with higher rates during the COVID‑19 pandemic.

📊 Key Statistics
• Anxiety disorders affect ~31.9% of adolescents (lifetime prevalence).
• Median age of onset: 11 years.
• Girls are 1.5‑2× more likely to develop anxiety than boys.
• Only 20‑30% receive treatment.
Sources: NIMH, WHO, DOH
🎯 Importance of Early Intervention
Untreated anxiety leads to academic underachievement, social isolation, substance abuse, and increased risk of depression and suicide. Early detection and treatment improve outcomes.
⚠️ Key point: Adolescence is a critical period for onset of anxiety disorders; screening should be routine in primary care.

Types of Anxiety Disorders (DSM‑5)

DisorderDescriptionKey Features
Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) Excessive worry about various events for ≥6 monthsRestlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, muscle tension, sleep disturbance
Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD)Marked fear of social situations where one may be scrutinizedAvoidance of social interactions, fear of embarrassment, physical symptoms (blushing, sweating)
Panic DisorderRecurrent unexpected panic attacksPalpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, fear of dying or losing control
Separation Anxiety DisorderDevelopmentally inappropriate fear of separation from attachment figuresDistress when separated, worry about harm to loved ones, school refusal, nightmares
Specific PhobiaIntense fear of a specific object or situation (e.g., animals, heights, blood)Immediate anxiety upon exposure, avoidance, significant distress or impairment
Mnemonic: “GAD, SAD, Panic, Separation, Phobia” – common adolescent anxiety disorders.

Clinical Features & Risk Factors

🩸 Physical Symptoms
• Palpitations, sweating, trembling
• Shortness of breath, chest tightness
• Nausea, abdominal distress
• Dizziness, lightheadedness
• Muscle tension, headaches
🧠 Cognitive/Emotional Symptoms
• Excessive worry, rumination
• Difficulty concentrating
• Irritability, restlessness
• Fear of losing control or dying
• Avoidance of feared situations
⚠️ Risk Factors
• Genetics (family history)
• Temperament (behavioral inhibition)
• Trauma or stressful life events
• Parental overprotection or criticism
• Peer victimization, bullying
• Chronic illness, academic pressure
📋 Red Flags for Referral
• Refusal to attend school for >2 weeks
• Self‑harm, suicidal ideation
• Severe weight loss (avoidant/restrictive intake)
• Functional impairment (cannot leave home)
• Symptoms lasting >6 months

Screening & Diagnosis

  • GAD‑7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder‑7): 7‑item self‑report scale for GAD; score ≥10 indicates moderate anxiety.
  • SCARED (Screen for Child Anxiety Related Emotional Disorders): Validated for children/adolescents; screens for panic, GAD, separation anxiety, social anxiety, school avoidance.
  • Clinical interview: Based on DSM‑5 criteria; rule out medical causes (hyperthyroidism, cardiac arrhythmias, caffeine/substance use).
  • Physical exam & labs: TSH, CBC, glucose, drug screen if indicated.
GAD‑7 scoring: 0‑4 minimal, 5‑9 mild, 10‑14 moderate, 15‑21 severe anxiety.

Management (CBT, Medications)

🧩 Psychotherapy (First‑line)
• Cognitive‑Behavioral Therapy (CBT) – most evidence‑based
• Exposure therapy for phobias
• Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)
• Interpersonal therapy for social anxiety
💊 Pharmacotherapy (Second‑line or adjunct)
• SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram) – FDA‑approved
• SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine)
• Benzodiazepines (short‑term only, risk of dependence)
• Buspirone for GAD
SSRI warning: Black box warning for increased suicidal ideation in young adults; monitor closely during first weeks of treatment.

Nursing Care & Interventions

  • Therapeutic communication: Use active listening, open‑ended questions, validate feelings. Avoid reassurance (e.g., “Don’t worry”).
  • Relaxation techniques: Teach deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery, mindfulness.
  • Safety: Assess for suicidal ideation, self‑harm, and protective factors. Provide safe environment.
  • Health education: Explain anxiety as a treatable condition. Educate about medication adherence, side effects, and importance of therapy.
  • School support: Collaborate with school counselor; implement 504 plan or IEP if needed.
Mnemonic “B.R.E.A.T.H.E.” – Breathing, Relaxation, Empathy, Assessment, Teaching, Hope, Education.

Prevention & Coping Strategies

  • Universal prevention: School‑based programs teaching coping skills, resilience, emotional regulation.
  • Targeted prevention: Interventions for high‑risk adolescents (children of anxious parents, bullying victims).
  • Lifestyle factors: Regular physical activity, adequate sleep, balanced diet, limiting caffeine and screen time.
  • Parental involvement: Reduce criticism, encourage autonomy, model healthy coping.
Mnemonic “C.O.P.E.” – Coping skills, Open communication, Physical activity, Education.
